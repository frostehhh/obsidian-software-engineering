Estimating things give no value.

Estimations are always guesses and are always wrong. Sure we can observe past behavior to help guess future changes, but in the end we're always guessing. We assume these estimates are completely valid, but deep inside we know that they are quite bullshit.

"Excessive or irrational schedules are probably the single most destructive influence in all of software."

Assuming a binding contract on an estimate that for exampple a feature will be done in 2 months results in chaos.

We just don't know how to estimate accurately. Get over it. Even when we do estimate, what's the point of getting them wrong most of the time?

Building the wrong thing on time and within budget doesn't buy you much.
> [!question] 
>  How can I relate this to what's happening in Lexagle?

Story points were invented to obfuscate duration so that certain managers would not pressure the team over estimates.

Velocity is incorrectly interpreted as a measure of efficiency. It should be a ratio of ideal time : actual time.

Developers should control their time. The business side decides what to build. In other words, nobody should order anybody around anymore.

If you don't have a set schedule, what do the managers do? The role of management is to support the team. Management should help the teams do what they need to do.

Any activity that doesn't directly contribute to putting something of value into the customer's hands is waste. All time spent estimating stuff is going to waste. That just lessens the time for development. by a lot.

You need to do accurate projects(not estimates). 

With estimates, you don't know that you're in trouble until the estimates slip so much that it's so obvious that you're in trouble, which happens later in the project lifecycle.

What you need to do instead, is count the number of stories and make projections. 20 seconds of effort as opposed to estimating, which can take hours. Predictive power of counting is identical to predictive power of estimates.

> In Lexagle's case, I think this can relate to how many tasks we have in our task breakdown or maybe the number of actual user stories that product writes.
